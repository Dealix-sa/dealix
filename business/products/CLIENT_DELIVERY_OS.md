# Client Delivery OS

## Positioning

Dealix builds a client delivery operating system for Saudi B2B companies — ensuring every project, service, or engagement is delivered with a documented SOP, progress tracking, proof of work, and a professional handoff that turns one-time projects into recurring relationships.

## Problem

Saudi B2B companies deliver work but cannot prove it. Projects start with enthusiasm, drift into confusion, and end with unclear deliverables. The client does not know what is happening. The team does not know what is due. There is no progress report, no proof of work, no professional handoff. The client is unsatisfied not because the work is bad, but because the experience was unmanaged. This kills referrals and repeat business.

## Ideal Customer

- Saudi B2B company delivering projects or services to clients (not pure product sales)
- Company with 5+ active client engagements at any time
- Founder who wants professional delivery without building a PMO from scratch
- Team struggling with scope, timelines, client communication, and handoff
- Industry: marketing agencies, training providers, B2B services, logistics, legal/accounting, IT services

## Objection Handling

**Objection 1: "We deliver fine, clients are happy."**
Response: If clients are happy, how many come back for a second engagement, and how many refer you? Happy is not the same as managed. The question is whether you can scale delivery quality as you grow, and whether you can prove it if a client disputes. A delivery OS protects good work from being undermined by a bad experience.

**Objection 2: "Our team is too small for this level of process."**
Response: This is designed for small teams. The process is lightweight — a pipeline, a weekly report, a proof template, and a handoff document. It adds 15 minutes per engagement per week and saves hours in confusion, disputes, and lost referrals. Small teams benefit most because they have less margin for error.

**Objection 3: "Clients do not care about reports, they care about results."**
Response: Results without visibility feel like results without results. If the client cannot see progress, they assume nothing is happening. The report is not the result — it is the evidence of the result. Clients who see progress trust the process, complain less, and refer more.

## Delivery SOP

1. Intake call (60 min): map delivery process, identify active engagements, understand pain points
2. Diagnosis (Day 1–2): audit current delivery, identify gaps in tracking, communication, handoff
3. Blueprint (Day 2–3): delivery SOP, templates, pipeline structure — client approval
4. Build (Day 3–7): delivery pipeline, progress report template, proof-of-work template, communication log
5. QA (Day 7): internal review of templates, test with a sample engagement
6. UAT (Day 7–8): operations lead reviews, tests with a real engagement
7. Launch (Day 8): pipeline live, weekly reporting begins
8. Training (Day 8–9): team trained on SOP, templates, proof-of-work, handoff
9. Proof (Day 14 and Day 30): delivery report, on-time rate, client feedback, handoff completion
10. Handoff (Day 30): delivery playbook, retainer decision

## Risks and Limitations

- Delivery quality depends on the team's actual work, not just the tracking system
- Team adoption is critical; if the team does not update the pipeline, visibility is lost
- Client engagement with progress reports varies; some clients will not read them
- Scope creep is a people problem; the system surfaces it but cannot prevent it alone
- Proof-of-work adds time to delivery; teams may resist the documentation overhead
- Integration with existing project management tools may require customization
