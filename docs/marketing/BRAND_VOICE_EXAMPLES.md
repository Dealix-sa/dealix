# Brand Voice Examples

> DEALIX — INTELLIGENT DEALS. REAL GROWTH.
> Concrete examples that calibrate the Dealix voice across channels.
> Each example has an "approved" form and the "rejected" form it
> replaces, with the rule that triggered the rejection.

This document is a calibration reference for the Content Strategist
agent, the Brand Guardian agent, and human operators. Examples are
synthesised; they do not name real buyers.

## Voice Calibration in One Paragraph

Dealix sounds like a principal engineer who has been a product
marketer for a decade and refuses to bullshit a buyer. Sober,
specific, refusal-aware, evidence-bearing. The voice trusts the
reader. The voice does not chase clicks. The voice in Arabic is a
peer of the voice in English, not a translation.

## Refusal Lists

**Approved:**

- We do not promise revenue, sales, or meetings.
- We do not execute external sends on your behalf.
- We do not publish anything about you without recorded approval.
- We do not commit pricing outside our offer documents.

- "Limitations apply. See terms and conditions." (R-COPY-014, the
  rejected version hides the refusal in a footer rather than

## LinkedIn Posts — Operating Lesson Pillar

**Approved:**

> A buyer asked us last week if we could "just send the outreach"
> for them, no approval step. We declined.
>
> Here is why.
>
> Outreach without an approval step is outreach without a refusal
> step. The first time a draft targets someone on the suppression
> list, or carries a sentence the buyer would not have said, the
> trust is gone. The shortcut is more expensive than the work.
>
> We draft. The buyer approves. The buyer's team sends. The audit
> trail is clean.

**Rejected:**

> Just closed a $100k deal in 14 days using AI. Here's how you
> can too. (Thread incoming.) (R-COPY-001, R-COPY-004, R-COPY-002,
> R-COPY-006.)

## LinkedIn Posts — Governance Teardown Pillar

**Approved:**

>

## Voice Tells (Patterns That Indicate Drift)

The Brand Guardian flags drafts that contain:

- More than one exclamation mark per long-form piece.
- More than one rhetorical question per short-form post.
- Multiple "amazing", "incredible", "powerful", "transformative" in
  a single document.
- A list that includes "and more!" as a final item.
- A headline that contains a number followed by "ways to".
- A sentence that begins with "imagine if".

## Why Concrete Examples

Rules describe boundaries; examples teach calibration. A drafter
working from rules alone tends to over-correct (every sentence
becomes a refusal list) or under-correct (the rules are read as
suggestions). Examples close the gap. They show the actual texture
of Dealix copy — sober, specific, refusal-aware, evidence-bearing,
in both Arabic and English.
