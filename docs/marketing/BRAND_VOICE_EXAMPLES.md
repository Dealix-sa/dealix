# Brand Voice Examples

Concrete examples of Dealix voice — what to say, what not to say, and why.

## 1. Hero / claim (good)

> Intelligent Deals. Real Growth.
> Saudi B2B revenue intelligence with AI assistance and human approval on every outbound move.

Why: states the tagline, names the buyer (Saudi B2B), names the doctrine (human approval), avoids superlatives.

## 1b. Hero / claim (bad)

> Dealix — the world's first fully autonomous AI sales platform that 10x's revenue.

Why bad: "world's first", "fully autonomous", "10x", "platform". Multiple doctrine violations.

## 2. LinkedIn post — Trust pillar (good)

> 12 outbound drafts ready. None will leave the system until I approve them.
> That's the doctrine: AI drafts, founder approves. One kill switch halts everything.
> If your motion can't survive that constraint, we're not for you.

Why: numerate ("12"), doctrine-led, calm, refusal explicit, ≤ 220 words.

## 3. LinkedIn post — Growth pillar (good)

> A Saudi B2B agency last sprint:
> Account scoring: 248 → 31 (after the model).
> Drafts queued: 18.
> Approved (after edits): 12.
> Replies in 14 days: 4.
> Meetings booked: 2.
> No surprise. Just the operating maths.

Why: specific numbers, specific time window, no causality claim ("we didn't 10x anyone").

## 4. Outreach email — first touch (good)

> Subject: KSA logistics — open pipeline question
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I noticed [observation, e.g. your post about KSA mega-projects pipeline]. A few KSA logistics CCOs we've worked with describe the same picture: RFP-heavy pipeline, top-of-funnel thin.
>
> If a 7-day diagnostic (bilingual, founder-approved drafts, kill switch on your side) is useful, happy to share a sample.
>
> No automation. Approval is on you.
>
> — [Founder name]

Why: cites observation, names the segment, names the offer, names the trust posture, ≤ 110 words.

## 5. Outreach email — refusal of fully-automated request (good)

> Thanks for the note. We can't operate a fully autonomous outbound for you — we draft, you approve, every time. That's the doctrine. If that constraint works, we'd love to help. If not, no hard feelings.

Why: refuses cleanly, restates doctrine, offers an alternative path (not silence).

## 6. Proposal — executive summary (good)

> Engagement: Revenue Sprint (7 days). Bilingual.
> Promise: a diagnostic of [Customer]'s outbound revenue motion, a 10-draft pack, and a sector-relevant proof pack.
> What we will NOT do: send anything externally without [Customer]'s approval, make pricing or contract commitments on [Customer]'s behalf, claim a guaranteed outcome.
> Price: 2,500 SAR. 7 days. Founder shadow session included.

Why: states the offer, states the refusal, states the price and time-box, no superlatives.

## 7. Console microcopy — approval queue (good)

- "Approve" — verb-first.
- "Hold" — verb-first.
- "Reject" — verb-first.
- "Kill switch armed." — status statement, no exclamation.
- "Nothing leaves the system until you decide." — doctrine reminder.

## 8. Refusal of a sector (good)

> We're not pursuing payday-lending introductions, even paid. The sector is on our refusal list. Happy to refer you to a partner who works there.

Why: clean refusal, explains the reason, offers an alternative.

## 9. Founder daily LinkedIn (good)

> Today's observation: 4 KSA B2B founders this week asked the same question — "How do we get our team off Excel pipelines without HubSpot pain?"
> Tomorrow's draft will answer it. Bilingual. Cited.

Why: observational, numerate, promises a deliverable.

## 10. What never appears

- "🚀", "🔥", any emoji.
- "!!!", any exclamation marks.
- "We unlock the potential of your revenue."
- "Game-changing AI that revolutionises Saudi B2B."
- "World's first revenue operating system."
- "Cutting-edge cutting-edge cutting-edge."
