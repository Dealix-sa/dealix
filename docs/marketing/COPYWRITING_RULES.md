# Copywriting Rules

> DEALIX — INTELLIGENT DEALS. REAL GROWTH.
> The rules every piece of Dealix copy must obey, every time.
> Enforced by the Brand Guardian agent and the claims-safety eval.

Copywriting rules at Dealix are not stylistic preferences. They are
the operating constraints that keep Dealix from drifting into
guaranteed-outcome marketing, manufactured urgency, or claims that
cannot be defended. The rules below apply to every channel: landing
pages, email, LinkedIn, newsletter, sector reports, sales scripts,
proposal copy, and product UI strings.

## Operating Principles

- Copy is evidence-bearing. A claim travels with its source.
- Copy is refusal-aware. What Dealix will not do is part of what
  Dealix says.
- Copy is sober. Sober is not the same as boring. Sober means
  serious without being grim, confident without being grand.
- Copy is testable. Any claim that cannot be tested by an eval is
  rewritten until it can.

## Rule Set

### R-COPY-001 — No guaranteed outcomes

Forbidden tokens (English):
"guaranteed", "promise", "we will get you", "secure your", "lock in".

Forbidden tokens (Arabic):
أضمن لك، نضمن لك، نعدك، نلتزم بتحقيق، نضمن تحقيق.

Replacement pattern: describe artefacts, queues, drafts, and
decisions. "We produce a Diagnostic Brief and a Scorecard." "We
queue outreach drafts for your approval."

### R-COPY-002 — No magnitude claims without method

Forbidden patterns: "10x", "3x", "double your", "up to N%",
"N% increase" — when no method or evidence is cited.

Replacement pattern: describe the loop and the artefact. If a
specific number is essential, state the method and the source.

### R-COPY-003 — No manufactured urgency

Forbidden patterns: "act now", "limited time", "spots filling up",
"only N seats left", countdown clocks.

Replacement pattern: state the next-step artefact and a realistic
review window. The buyer's calendar decides urgency, not Dealix.

### R-COPY-004 — No unapproved proof

Forbidden patterns: customer logos, customer names, screenshots,
quotes, or numbers that are not recorded as `approved` in the trust
ledger.

Replacement pattern: anonymised data points, governance teardowns,
sector reports.

### R-COPY-005 — No competitor comparison

Forbidden patterns: "we are like X but better", "unlike X",
"X is dead", and any side-by-side comparison.

Replacement pattern: Dealix anti-positioning. State what Dealix is
not, in terms of category not vendor.

### R-COPY-006 — No vanity metrics

Forbidden patterns: "trusted by N companies" without method,
"million-dollar pipelines closed" without source, follower counts
displayed in copy.

Replacement pattern: describe the working artefacts a buyer can
expect.

### R-COPY-007 — No corporate filler

Forbidden patterns: "world-class", "best-in-class", "leading",
"cutting-edge", "next-generation", "synergy", "leverage" (as verb),
"unlock", "empower".

Replacement pattern: use plain operative language. "We operate the
queue." "We file the audit." "We refuse this request."

### R-COPY-008 — No emojis

The Dealix voice does not use emojis in any external-facing copy.
Internal queues and dashboards may use semantic icons; external
text does not.

### R-COPY-009 — Active voice, short sentences

Default to active voice. Default to sentences under 25 words. Long
sentences are allowed when they carry structure; long sentences as
ornament are not.

### R-COPY-010 — Sentence-case headlines

Headlines use sentence case, not title case. No all-caps copy
except for the DEALIX wordmark itself and the tagline.

### R-COPY-011 — Saudi B2B grounding

Where copy references a market context, that context is Saudi B2B
with concrete sector or regulatory examples. Generic "global" or
"international" framing is replaced with specifics.

### R-COPY-012 — Bilingual parity

Where copy ships in Arabic and English, both versions are peer
artefacts. Arabic is not a translation; Arabic is written by an
Arabic-fluent operator. Brand voice calibration applies to both.

### R-COPY-013 — Pricing posture, not pricing numbers

Copy describes how Dealix prices (band-based, founder-approved). It
does not display rung pricing in public-facing channels. Pricing
numbers live in the offer document.

### R-COPY-014 — Refusal visibility

Long-form copy (landing pages, sector reports, founder long-form)
must contain a refusal list. Short-form copy (posts, emails) may
reference the universal refusals without listing all of them.

### R-COPY-015 — Trust ledger references

Where copy describes a piece of evidence, it links to the source
(audit ledger entry, sector report, governance teardown). Copy that
references evidence without a link is flagged.

## Tone Calibration

The Brand Guardian eval scores draft copy against a tone vector:

- **Sober vs. theatrical.** Target: sober. Theatrical phrasing is
  flagged.
- **Specific vs. generic.** Target: specific. Generic claims are
  flagged.
- **Refusal-aware vs. avoidant.** Target: refusal-aware. Copy that
  avoids stating refusals is flagged.
- **Evidence-bearing vs. opinion-only.** Target: evidence-bearing.
  Opinion-only copy is allowed in founder voice but labelled.

## Layout Conventions

- Headlines: 6–12 words for landing pages; 4–8 words for posts.
- Sub-headlines: one sentence.
- Paragraphs: 2–4 sentences for landing pages; 1–3 sentences for
  posts.
- Bullet lists: ideally 3–5 items; never single-item bullets.
- Tables: used for any contract-shaped content (rungs, refusals,
  rules).

## Approval Flow for Copy

1. Drafter (Content Strategist, Distribution Operator, founder)
   writes the copy.
2. Brand Guardian agent runs claims-safety and brand-voice evals.
3. Trust Guardian agent runs proof-safety eval.
4. Drafter resolves flags.
5. Founder approves or returns.
6. Approved copy is published / sent manually.

The Brand Guardian agent operates at A1 — it does not edit copy
autonomously. It surfaces flags and rewrites; the human accepts or
rejects.

## Eval Tests

The claims-safety eval includes:

- Regex passes for guaranteed-outcome tokens (English and Arabic).
- Magnitude-claim pattern detection.
- Urgency-trigger detection.
- Proof reference cross-check against the trust ledger.
- Vanity-metric detection.
- Corporate-filler detection.

A draft that fails any test is returned with the failure reasons.

## Localisation Notes

Arabic copy follows the same rules with one additional concern:
mixed-language dialect. Dealix copy uses Modern Standard Arabic with
Saudi business idiom where appropriate. Heavy dialect (purely
colloquial Saudi spoken Arabic) is avoided in formal artefacts but
may appear in short videos or social posts where it matches the
audience.

## Style Notes That Are Not Rules

These are conventions; they are not enforced by eval, but they are
preferred.

- Use the second person ("you") in landing pages and emails. Use
  the first person plural ("we") when speaking as Dealix.
- Avoid the editorial "we" in product descriptions. Describe the
  artefact directly.
- Avoid parenthetical asides in headlines.
- Prefer "decline" to "refuse" in customer-facing copy where
  warmth is warranted; both are allowed.

## Failure Modes

- Copy published with a guaranteed-outcome token. Failure: eval
  miss. Copy is retracted; eval is upgraded.
- Copy published with an unapproved customer name. Failure: proof
  safety miss. Copy is retracted; trust ledger records the
  incident.
- Copy approved by founder despite a flag. Failure: founder
  override. The override is logged, with the founder's rationale, in
  the trust ledger.
- Copy that consistently scores low on the tone vector. Failure:
  drafter calibration. Brand Guardian publishes a calibration memo
  and the drafter is re-onboarded.

## Anti-Patterns

- "It is just a small phrase." No phrase is too small to break the
  rules.
- "The competitor says it." Dealix is not bound to the competitor's
  marketing posture.
- "It tested well in A/B." The rules are not subject to A/B
  testing. Within the rules, wording can be tested.
- "It's for a closed group." Internal copy can be looser; if it
  could ever go external, the rules apply.

## Why a Rule Set, Not a Style Guide

A style guide describes preferences. A rule set describes the
boundaries inside which Dealix is willing to be Dealix. The rules
are part of the trust contract. A buyer who reads Dealix copy and
finds it free of guarantees, free of manufactured urgency, and full
of refusals has already encountered the operating system at work.
That is the conversion that matters.
