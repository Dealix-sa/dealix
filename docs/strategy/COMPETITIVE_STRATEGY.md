# Competitive Strategy

> How we win against each class of competitor.
> Updated when a real deal goes head-to-head and we learn something.

## Competitor Classes

### 1. Local marketing agencies
**They look like:** Riyadh/Jeddah-based, retainer SAR 10K–30K/mo, do creative + sometimes outreach.

**Their strength:** Existing client relationships, Arabic-native, in-market.

**Their weakness:** Activity-based pricing, no productized scope, no evidence pack, no system handover.

**How we win:** Lead with productized Sprint (SAR 499) → prove outcome → propose Managed Ops at lower retainer cost with **more visibility**. Show the evidence pack they can't produce.

### 2. Generalist AI consultancies
**They look like:** Cloud partners or boutique AI shops, SAR 50K+/mo, big PowerPoints, slow.

**Their strength:** Brand, enterprise relationships.

**Their weakness:** No mid-market motion, no productized pricing, slow procurement.

**How we win:** Be the **fast, small, evidenced** alternative. We don't compete for the same buyer — we get the mid-market the consultancies don't service.

### 3. DIY ChatGPT + spreadsheets
**They look like:** A founder who watched a YouTube video and is using GPT-4 for lead research.

**Their strength:** Free.

**Their weakness:** No trust gates, no audit, no continuity, no compounding. Hallucinations leak to customers.

**How we win:** Show the **first time it bites them** (an embarrassing message, a wrong claim). Position Dealix as the "with guard rails" version. Free Diagnostic exposes the gap.

### 4. SaaS RevOps tools (Apollo, HubSpot, etc.)
**They look like:** Subscription tools, USD pricing, English-only UI, no service layer.

**Their strength:** Polished UI, scale, integrations.

**Their weakness:** Tool-only — no one does the work. Saudi SMB rarely operates them well. SAR pricing friction.

**How we win:** Be the **operating layer** on top. We don't compete with the tool — we operate the tool for the buyer. Some buyers will use both; that's fine.

### 5. Internal hire (SDR, RevOps manager)
**They look like:** SAR 8K–25K/month employee, 30-day notice, no system.

**Their strength:** Dedicated, in-language, in-culture.

**Their weakness:** Single point of failure, slow to onboard, expensive total cost (salary + tools + training).

**How we win:** Position Dealix as "the system you'd want the next SDR to inherit". Lower total cost. Founder can hire the SDR later, and Dealix becomes the playbook they run.

## Win Themes (use in proposals)

1. **Productized, not project-priced** — fixed scope, fixed price
2. **Evidence-backed, not opinion-backed** — every claim has a source
3. **Founder-approved, not full-auto** — humans on every external action
4. **Arabic-first market knowledge** — sector playbooks are local
5. **Compounding system, not consulting hours** — what we build stays with you

## Loss Themes (track and counter)

When we lose, log it in `pipeline/win_loss_log.md` (private). Common loss patterns to watch:

- "Price too high vs DIY" → counter with Free Diagnostic to expose DIY cost
- "Too small a name" → counter with case study + advisor reference
- "We want full automation" → walk away (not ICP)
- "We need someone in-office" → propose hybrid + monthly on-site visit (Managed Ops only)

## Strategy Refresh Triggers

Update this doc when:
- We lose 2 deals to the same competitor in a month
- A competitor changes pricing publicly
- A new entrant enters Saudi mid-market
- A buyer cites a competitor we'd never heard of

## What We Refuse To Do To Win

- Discount below the productized floor (signals weakness)
- Mock a competitor in public (low-class, lasting damage)
- Promise outcomes we don't have evidence for
- Copy a competitor's positioning (we play a different game on purpose)
