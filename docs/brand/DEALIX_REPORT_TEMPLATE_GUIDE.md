# DEALIX Report Template Guide

> Wordmark: **DEALIX**
> Tagline: **INTELLIGENT DEALS. REAL GROWTH.**

This document defines the Dealix sector report template — structure,
disclosure notes, and the rules that keep our reports defensible against
regulators, journalists, and informed buyers. A Dealix report carries
weight only if everything in it can be defended.

---

## 1. What a Dealix report is

A Dealix sector report is a focused, source-disclosed brief on a Saudi
B2B sector. It typically covers a single sector (e.g. Saudi B2B
logistics, healthcare, professional services) for a specific time
window (e.g. Q1 2026). It is **not** a generic industry overview, **not**
a marketing whitepaper, and **not** a press release dressed as research.

Reports earn their reader. A reader who finishes a Dealix report should
feel they learned a defensible fact about their market, not that they
were sold to.

---

## 2. Report tiers

| Tier | Length | Audience | Distribution |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Micro-brief | 3–5 pages | LinkedIn / sales follow-up | Open |
| Sector report | 12–20 pages | Sector buyers, partners | Gated by email |
| Annual outlook | 30–50 pages | Board, partners, press | Gated, embargoed |

Each tier shares the same structure and disclosure discipline. They
differ in scope and depth.

---

## 3. Canonical structure

Every report — regardless of tier — has these sections in this order:

1. Cover
2. Executive summary
3. Methodology and sample
4. The pattern (the central observation)
5. Sub-patterns by sub-sector or geography
6. What is changing
7. What is **not** changing (the persistent constraints)
8. Implications for operators
9. Limitations and caveats
10. About Dealix (one paragraph)
11. Sources and contributors
12. Disclosure notes

Optional appendices may be added (raw data, glossary). Never remove a
canonical section.

---

## 4. Section 1 — Cover

- Deep Navy background.
- Report title in Inter Bold, large.
- Sub-title in Inter Medium.
- Publication date.
- Version (`v1`, `v2`...).
- Small wordmark + tagline at the bottom.
- A single sober line at the bottom of the cover: "Sample size:
  [N]. Time window: [from–to]. Methodology: see page [X]."

The cover does **not** include a hero photo, a customer logo, or a
splashy promise.

---

## 5. Section 2 — Executive summary

A one-page executive summary with:

- 3–5 short paragraphs.
- 3 numbered key takeaways at the top.
- Each takeaway carries a denominator or a "qualitative finding"
  marker.

The executive summary is the most-read part of the report. It must be
honest: if the data is thin, the summary says so.

---

## 6. Section 3 — Methodology and sample

A short methodology section that names:

- The sample (how many companies, what sub-sectors).
- The time window.
- The collection method (interviews, anonymized log data, public
  filings).
- The exclusions (what we excluded and why).
- The author and reviewer.

The methodology section is **mandatory**. A report without methodology
does not ship. This section also names any data that was provided
under NDA and is therefore aggregated, not cited individually.

---

## 7. Section 4 — The pattern

The central observation of the report. Usually 1–3 pages.

- Headline (Inter Bold `3xl`): the pattern in one sentence.
- Diagram: the pattern visualized (bars, flow, table).
- Body: 4–8 short paragraphs explaining the pattern.
- Pull-quote (optional): an anonymized buyer quote that
  illustrates the pattern. Only included if approval is logged.

This section is where the report earns its keep. The pattern must be
defensible against the methodology section.

---

## 8. Section 5 — Sub-patterns

If the sample supports it, break the pattern down by sub-sector,
geography, or company size.

- One sub-section per sub-pattern.
- Each sub-pattern carries its own sample size.
- If a sub-sample is too small to support a sub-pattern, say so
  explicitly: "Insufficient sample (n<5) for confident sub-sector
  pattern."

We do **not** report a sub-pattern just because the chart looks better
with more colors.

---

## 9. Section 6 — What is changing

A forward-looking section, 1–2 pages. Names:

- The macro factors that are changing (regulatory, economic,
  technological).
- The micro factors that are changing (buyer behavior, hiring
  patterns).
- The factors we are watching but cannot yet conclude on.

Forward-looking statements use cautious language: "appears to be",
"observed in early signals", "may". They do **not** use "will" without
evidence.

---

## 10. Section 7 — What is not changing

A counter-balancing section. The persistent constraints — regulation,
buyer skepticism, market structure — that operators must continue to
operate within. This section is **uniquely Dealix**: it earns trust
because it does not pretend everything is in motion.

---

## 11. Section 8 — Implications for operators

A practical section. For each pattern, name:

- What an operator might do differently this quarter.
- What an operator should **not** do (e.g. "do not chase a sub-sector
  with n<5 in our sample").
- A check-list of 3–5 questions an operator should ask their team.

This section is **not** a sales pitch. It does not say "use Dealix to
do X". It says "here is what informed operators do".

---

## 12. Section 9 — Limitations and caveats

A short, honest section that names:

- The sample size limits.
- The geographic and temporal limits.
- The selection bias (who we talked to and who we did not).
- The reproducibility constraints (some data is under NDA).

A report without this section is a marketing document, not a report.

---

## 13. Section 10 — About Dealix

One paragraph. No more.

> Dealix is the Saudi B2B Revenue Operating System. We help Saudi B2B
> teams move from fragmented signals to closed deals — with evidence,
> with approvals, and with no automated external sending. Every
> Dealix engagement is trust-gated by default.

This section does not pitch the offer ladder. The reader can find
that on the marketing site.

---

## 14. Section 11 — Sources and contributors

- Sources: cite every public source (government data, regulator
  publications, public filings). Inline citations as `[n]`,
  collected in an end-of-report list.
- Contributors: name the analyst, the reviewers, and the editor.
- Anonymous contributors: list them anonymously with their sector and
  role (e.g. "COO, Saudi B2B logistics, anonymized at request").

---

## 15. Section 12 — Disclosure notes

The most important section legally. It carries:

- **Approval disclosures.** Any cited customer is cited with logged
  approval. Any uncited customer remained anonymous.
- **Funding / interest disclosure.** If Dealix has a commercial
  relationship with any cited entity, it is named here.
- **PDPL disclosure.** "All personal data referenced in this report
  is processed under PDPL-aligned consent. Aggregated data does not
  re-identify individuals."
- **Forward-looking statement disclosure.** "Forward-looking
  statements are observations and may not materialize. Dealix does
  not guarantee outcomes."
- **No guarantee disclosure.** "Nothing in this report is a
  guarantee of revenue, sales, or business outcomes. Operators should
  apply their own judgment."

This section is non-optional and does not get trimmed for length.

---

## 16. Visual rules

- Pages: A4 portrait, single column, generous margins.
- Body type: Inter Regular `base`, 1.5× line height.
- Headings: Inter Bold (h1) / Inter Semibold (h2, h3).
- Section dividers: a thin Soft Silver rule.
- Tables: alternating row shading in `#F3F5F8`, Inter Semibold
  headers.
- Charts: limited palette per `DEALIX_COLOR_SYSTEM.md` section 7.
  Each chart carries a title, a denominator, and a source.
- Footer on every page: report title, page number, version, date.
- Wordmark in the top-right of every page (top-left in RTL).
- Cover and section dividers may use Deep Navy backgrounds; the body
  stays White for print legibility.

---

## 17. Chart rules

- Every chart has a title.
- Every chart has a denominator (`n=...`).
- Every chart has a source citation.
- No 3D charts. No exploded pies.
- Bar charts default to horizontal for readability on long sector
  names.
- Time-series charts use a clear x-axis time format.

---

## 18. Naming and storage

Files follow the marketing asset naming convention:

- `dealix_report_{sector}_{quarter}_{locale}_{version}.pdf`
- Example: `dealix_report_logistics_q1_2026_en_v1.pdf`
- Example: `dealix_report_logistics_q1_2026_ar_v1.pdf`

Stored in `assets/brand/marketing/reports/`. PDFs are PDF/A-1b with
embedded fonts.

---

## 19. Release process

1. Analyst drafts the report.
2. Internal review: brand director, founder, sector reviewer.
3. Legal pass: disclosure notes, customer references, regulatory
   citations.
4. External reviewer (optional): a sector expert with NDA.
5. Final draft frozen at `vN-final-draft`.
6. Publication on the chosen date. Distribution is gated for sector
   and annual reports; open for micro-briefs.
7. Post-publication corrections published as `vN.1` with a visible
   change-log on the last page.

---

## 20. Bilingual reports

For Saudi B2B audiences, reports may be released bilingual (Arabic
primary) or in two parallel files. The Arabic version:

- Is original copy, reviewed by an Arabic-native analyst.
- Uses Arabic-Indic numerals in prose, ASCII in tables and charts.
- Carries the same methodology and disclosure structure.

## 22. Report checklist (pre-publish)

- [ ] Methodology and sample disclosed.
- [ ] Every chart has `n=...` and a source.
- [ ] Forward-looking statements use cautious language.
- [ ] Limitations and caveats present and honest.
- [ ] Customer references have logged approvals.
- [ ] Disclosure notes section complete.
- [ ] Legal pass complete.
- [ ] Bilingual version prepared if announced.
- [ ] PDF/A-1b export with embedded fonts.
- [ ] Filename follows convention.
- [ ] Filed in proof ledger.

A report that fails any item does not publish.
