# DEALIX Sales Deck Guide

> Wordmark: **DEALIX**
> Tagline: **INTELLIGENT DEALS. REAL GROWTH.**

This document defines the Dealix sales deck — the structure, the slide
inventory, and the rules that keep the deck on-brand and defensible. The
deck is the primary face Dealix presents in a 45-minute meeting. It must
do three jobs at the same time: name the problem the buyer feels, name
Dealix as the category-appropriate answer, and earn the buyer's trust.

---

## 1. Deck stance

The deck is operator-built. It does not look like an investment-bank
pitch deck and it does not look like a SaaS-conference deck. It looks
like a senior Saudi B2B operator sat down with a buyer and walked them
through a sober plan.

Two principles:

1. **One idea per slide.** If a slide has two ideas, it becomes two
   slides.
2. **Evidence next to every claim.** A claim without evidence does not
   make it into the deck. If we cannot show the evidence, we soften the
   claim or remove it.

---

## 2. Deck structure (the arc)

The canonical deck arc:

1. Cover
2. The Saudi B2B pattern (the problem)
3. Why the current alternatives stall
4. The category — Saudi B2B Revenue OS
5. What Dealix actually does
6. The trust gate
7. Offer ladder
8. Pilot Sprint — what happens in 14 days
9. Evidence and references
10. Pricing band
11. Risk and what we never promise
12. Acceptance / next step

The deck is roughly 12–18 slides long. Shorter is better than longer.

---

## 3. Slide 1 — Cover

- Full-bleed Deep Navy.
- Centered wordmark + tagline.
- A single sober line under the tagline:
  "Saudi B2B Revenue Operating System".
- Bottom: prepared for [buyer], date, version.
- The cover does not include a hero photo or a sales tagline.

---

## 4. Slide 2 — The Saudi B2B pattern

The "problem" slide. One sentence, one diagram.

- Headline (Inter Bold `3xl`): "Saudi B2B deals stall in the same place
  every time."
- Diagram (Slate cards, Soft Silver edges): the typical Saudi B2B
  funnel with the stall point highlighted in Emerald Teal.
- Sub-line (Soft Silver, `sm`): "Pattern observed across [N]
  discoveries, Q[X] [year]. Sample size disclosed."

This slide opens the buyer's attention because they recognize the
pattern. It does **not** describe Dealix yet.

---

## 5. Slide 3 — Why current alternatives stall

A 2×2 table that names the alternatives and their stall reasons.

| Alternative | Stall reason |
| --- | --- |
| Scraping tools | Cold data, no approval gate, ZERO trust |
| Spam / blast tools | Brand damage, regulator risk |
| Generic CRM (HubSpot/Salesforce/Zoho) | Pipeline tracker, not pipeline mover |
| Generic agency | Outsourced, opaque, no audit trail |

This slide is a **competitive narrative slide** done in 60 seconds. Detail
lives in `COMPETITIVE_NARRATIVE.md` and `COMPETITOR_COMPARISON_MATRIX.md`.

---

## 6. Slide 4 — The category

Name the category we are creating:

- Headline (Inter Bold `3xl`): "Saudi B2B Revenue OS."
- Body (Inter Regular `md`): one paragraph that defines the category
  in operator language.
- Diagram on the right: a layered stack showing intelligence layer,
  approval layer, execution layer, evidence layer.

This slide is the category-creation moment. It positions Dealix not as
"better CRM" but as a different layer entirely.

---

## 7. Slide 5 — What Dealix actually does

Three columns, three nouns.

| Drafts | Approves | Records |
| --- | --- | --- |
| AI prepares the artifacts (deal maps, outbound drafts, follow-ups) | A human reviews and approves every external action | Every send and signature is recorded in the evidence ledger |

The slide is read in 20 seconds. It explicitly names the human-in-the-
loop pattern. This is the slide that closes the door on the "AI does it
all" misread.

---

## 8. Slide 6 — The trust gate

A single visual that shows the approval queue:

- Inbound signal → drafted message → **approval gate (highlighted)** →
  external send → ledger entry.
- The approval gate is the largest element on the slide.
- Caption: "No external action ships without a human approval, recorded
  with timestamp and owner."

This slide is non-negotiable. It is the slide that separates Dealix from
auto-outbound tools and protects the buyer's brand.

---

## 9. Slide 7 — Offer ladder

Three or four offers, side by side.

| Pilot Sprint (14 days) | Pilot Extension (+30 days) | Quarterly (90 days) | Annual (12 months) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Single use case | Second use case | Multi use case | Embedded |
| Fixed price | Fixed price | Subscription | Subscription |
| Per offer ladder | Per offer ladder | Per offer ladder | Per offer ladder |

The slide references the live offer ladder; specific numbers are quoted
in the proposal, not on the deck.

---

## 10. Slide 8 — Pilot Sprint detail

A 14-day timeline showing what happens each phase. Same content as the
proposal's timeline section, in slide form:

| Day 0–1 | Day 2–4 | Day 5–7 | Day 8–11 | Day 12–14 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Kickoff, sign DPA | Build deal map | Draft outbound | Approve & run | Review ledger |

The slide ends with a sober promise: "At the end of the sprint, you
have artifacts and a signed evidence ledger. You decide what comes
next."

---

## 11. Slide 9 — Evidence and references

This slide is conditional. It appears only if:

- The customer has approved publication.
- The approval is recorded in the proof ledger.
- The metric shown carries a denominator.

If those conditions are not met, the slide is replaced with:

- Headline: "Operating with a small cohort of Saudi B2B teams."
- Sub-line: "Signed pilots under written agreement. References shared
  on approval."

We do **not** show logos we have not earned the right to show.

---

## 12. Slide 10 — Pricing band

A simple visual showing the band for the offer most relevant to the
buyer:

- Headline: "Pilot Sprint — [SAR band], net-30, VAT separate."
- Inclusion list: 5 bullets summarizing scope.
- Exclusion list: 3 bullets summarizing what is not included.
- Validity: "This band is valid for 14 days from [date]."

Detailed pricing lives in the proposal. The deck shows the band so the
buyer can size the investment in the room.

---

## 13. Slide 11 — Risk and what we never promise

This is the **most counter-intuitive slide** in the deck. It is also the
slide that earns the most trust.

- Headline (Inter Semibold `3xl`): "What we do not promise."
- Three bullets:
  1. "We do not guarantee a specific revenue, sales, or meeting count.
     We deliver artifacts and a signed evidence ledger."
  2. "We do not publish your name, logo, or results without your written
     approval."
  3. "We do not auto-send. Every external action passes through your
     approval queue."

Saudi B2B buyers have been pitched a thousand decks promising guaranteed
outcomes. The deck that names what it will **not** promise stands out.

---

## 14. Slide 12 — Acceptance / next step

The closing slide. One question, one signature line.

- Headline: "Next step."
- Body: "Sign the Pilot Sprint proposal by [date]. We start [date].
  We review by [date]."
- A small "DEALIX — INTELLIGENT DEALS. REAL GROWTH." footer.

The slide does **not** include "questions?" — that is implicit in any
deck.

---

## 15. Optional slides (add only when needed)

| Slide | When to include |
| --- | --- |
| Sector data | When the buyer's sector has a publishable brief |
| Team | When the buyer has explicitly asked who is delivering |
| Compliance detail | When the buyer is in a regulated sector |
| Technical architecture | When the buyer is technical and asks |
| Integration map | When the buyer asks about existing tools |

Optional slides are inserted between the trust slide and the offer
ladder, never before the category slide.

---

## 16. Visual rules

- Slide background: Deep Navy or White, pick one and hold it across
  consecutive slides. A section break can flip the surface, but mid-
  section flips are jarring.
- Headlines: Inter Bold `3xl`–`4xl`.
- Body: Inter Regular `md`.
- Table cells: Inter Regular `sm`, alternating row shading on White
  slides.
- Wordmark in footer of every slide except the cover.
- Slide number in footer.
- No icon clutter; one diagram per content slide max.

---

## 17. Forbidden patterns

- "We will increase your sales by X%". Never.
- "Guaranteed X meetings in Y days". Never.
- Customer logos without approval. Never.
- A "trusted by" slide that lists logos we have not earned the right
  to list. Never.
- Stock photography of generic businesspeople. Never.
- Cluttered slides with 6+ bullets. Reduce to one idea.

---

## 18. Bilingual decks

The Arabic deck is structured identically but is original copy. Slide
order is identical. The deck is delivered as a separate file
(`dealix_deck_master_ar_vN.pdf`). A bilingual deck (Arabic primary,
English appendix) is offered when the buying committee is mixed.

---

## 19. Sales deck review checklist

Before the deck is taken into a meeting:

- [ ] Cover updated with buyer name and date.
- [ ] Pattern slide reflects the buyer's sector if known.
- [ ] Competitive slide named without ad hominem.
- [ ] Trust slide present and unedited.
- [ ] Pricing band matches the latest offer ladder.
- [ ] Risk slide present and unedited.
- [ ] No banned vocabulary anywhere.
- [ ] No unauthorized customer references.
- [ ] Bilingual version ready if buyer is Arabic-first.

The deck does not enter a meeting until this checklist is clean.
