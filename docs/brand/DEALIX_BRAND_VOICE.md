# DEALIX Brand Voice

> Wordmark: **DEALIX**
> Tagline: **INTELLIGENT DEALS. REAL GROWTH.**

This document defines the Dealix voice: how we sound, what we say, what we
never say, and how the same voice carries across English and Arabic. It is
the upstream source for every customer-facing sentence — site, deck,
proposal, email, signature, social.

If a sentence cannot pass this document, it does not ship.

---

## 1. Who is speaking

Dealix sounds like a senior Saudi B2B operator. Someone who has actually
closed deals in this market, who has been disappointed by martech vendors,
and who now builds the tool they wished they had. Calm, specific, sober.
Direct without being curt. Bilingual without being performative.

This voice is **one person, not a department**. We do not pluralize into a
"team" or hide behind "our platform". We address the reader directly and
take ownership of what we say.

---

## 2. Tone in four axes

We position the voice along four axes that bound how it can flex by
surface.

| Axis | Always | Never |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Confidence | Specific | Boastful |
| Warmth | Respectful | Effusive |
| Formality | Professional | Stiff |
| Energy | Steady | Hyped |

A board memo is more formal than a LinkedIn post — but both stay in this
quadrant. We do not write a board memo in stiff academic prose, and we do
not write a LinkedIn post in startup-bro cadence.

---

## 3. What we never say

These phrases — and any close paraphrase, in English or Arabic — are
forbidden on any Dealix surface.

### 3.1 Outcome guarantees
- "guaranteed revenue"
- "guaranteed sales"
- "guaranteed meetings"
- "guaranteed pipeline"
- "guaranteed leads"
- "guaranteed ROI"
- "we promise X revenue / sales / meetings / leads"
- "X% lift, guaranteed"
- "نضمن إيرادات / مبيعات / اجتماعات"

We cannot guarantee outcomes that depend on a buyer's actions. Saying we
can is dishonest, and Saudi enterprise buyers have learned to discount it
automatically.

### 3.2 Hype vocabulary
We avoid:
- "Revolutionary", "groundbreaking", "game-changing", "disruptive",
  "best-in-class", "world-class" (about ourselves).
- "10x", "100x" applied to a customer outcome.
- "Effortless", "magic", "instant" applied to revenue work.
- "Just one click and your pipeline fills" and its paraphrases.

### 3.3 Borrowed credibility
We do not publish a customer name, logo, quote, or metric without a
written approval recorded in the proof ledger. We do not say "trusted by
leading Saudi enterprises" if we cannot name them with approval. We do
not list logos we have not earned the right to list.

### 3.4 Automation overpromise
We do not advertise that the product sends external messages on its own.
The product drafts; humans approve. Our copy reflects that.

| Off-brand | On-brand |
| --- | --- |
| "AI sends emails for you" | "AI drafts, you approve, then it sends" |
| "Hands-off outbound" | "Operator-in-the-loop outbound" |
| "Auto-replies to your prospects" | "Drafts replies in seconds, you approve" |
| "Set it and forget it" | "Set it, review, ship" |

## 4. What we always do

### 4.1 Lead with the outcome
The first sentence of any surface names the outcome the reader cares
about. "Close more Saudi B2B deals with evidence" beats "Dealix is an
AI-powered platform that...".

### 4.2 Show the evidence
When we make a claim, we attach evidence in the same surface — a number
with a denominator, a screenshot with a date, a customer name with
approval, or an explicit "early signal, n=3" disclosure.

### 4.3 Name the constraint
We say what the product is **not**. "Dealix is not a scraper, not a spam
tool, not a generic CRM" beats a vague positioning sweep. Naming the
constraint earns trust.

### 4.4 Approval-first language
We refer to the approval queue explicitly. "Every external send passes
through a human approval queue" is a sentence we are happy to repeat.

### 4.5 Bilingual where it matters
Customer-facing surfaces serve Arabic-first audiences. We provide an
Arabic version that is original copy, not a literal translation.

## 5. English vocabulary

### Preferred verbs
*close, ship, evidence, approve, draft, route, surface, hold, gate,
record, audit, sign, deliver*

### Preferred nouns
*deal, pipeline, evidence, approval, ledger, sector, account, signal,
proof, queue, sprint, room*

### Avoided verbs
*revolutionize, transform (in the slogan sense), unleash, supercharge,
crush, hack, dominate*

### Avoided nouns
*solution (alone), synergy, paradigm, ecosystem (as filler), magic*

## 7. Voice by surface

| Surface | Tone shift | Length | Example opener |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Marketing hero | High confidence, low length | 1–2 lines | "Saudi B2B deals close on evidence, not promises." |
| Sales deck | Operator-to-operator | 1 idea per slide | "Three reasons Saudi B2B deals stall." |
| Proposal | Buyer-respectful, specific | Bulleted | "Scope: 14-day Pilot Sprint. Outcome: shipped artifacts, signed evidence." |
| Email (outbound) | Brief, named, no hype | <120 words | "We've been studying [sector] for the past 8 weeks. One pattern stands out." |
| LinkedIn post | Operator essay | 5–10 lines | "After 40 Saudi B2B discoveries, here's the pattern most decks miss." |
| Customer onboarding | Warm, specific, no jargon | Conversational | "Welcome. Here's what happens in the next 72 hours." |
| Support reply | Calm, accountable | As needed | "Thanks for flagging this. Here's what we changed and when." |
| Board memo | Sober, evidence-led | Structured | "Q-to-date: pipeline $X (n=Y deals); approvals: Z." |

## 8. Examples — English

### 8.1 On-brand
- "Dealix is the Saudi B2B revenue operating system. We help you close
  more deals with evidence, not promises."
- "Every external send passes through a human approval. The product
  drafts; you approve; we record."
- "In our first 14-day Pilot Sprint, we ship three artifacts: a deal
  map, an approval-queued outbound run, and a signed evidence ledger."

### 8.2 Off-brand (and the fix)
- Off: "Dealix guarantees 30% more sales in 60 days."
  Fix: "Customers using the 14-day Pilot Sprint report measurable
  pipeline lift. Results vary by sector; see methodology."
- Off: "AI automates your entire outbound effortlessly."
  Fix: "AI drafts your outbound. You approve. The product records."
- Off: "Trusted by the leading Saudi B2B brands."
  Fix: "Operating with a small cohort of Saudi B2B teams under signed
  pilot agreements. Logos shared on approval."

## 10. Reviewing copy — a short checklist

Before any sentence ships:

1. Does it claim a guaranteed revenue/sales/meeting outcome? If yes,
   revise.
2. Does it name a customer or metric without approval? If yes, hold.
3. Does it imply external sending without human approval? If yes,
   revise.
4. Does it use hype vocabulary (revolutionary, magic, effortless, 10x)?
   If yes, revise.
5. Is the outcome named in the first sentence?
6. Is the evidence (or its absence, honestly) named?
7. Does it have an Arabic equivalent on the same surface, if customer-
   facing?

A sentence that fails any of items 1–4 is off-brand and must be revised.

---

## 11. Voice ownership

The brand director owns the voice. The founder signs the voice on
high-stakes surfaces (homepage hero, deck cover, board memo). A bilingual
reviewer signs the Arabic copy when it is not authored by a native
speaker. Voice changes flow through a versioned update to this file —
not through scattered Slack messages.

---

## 12. Final principle

Be the brand that the Saudi B2B operator wishes they had been pitched by
five years ago. Calm, specific, bilingual, evidence-led, approval-gated.
That is enough. That is the brand.
