# DEALIX Proposal Template Guide

> Wordmark: **DEALIX**
> Tagline: **INTELLIGENT DEALS. REAL GROWTH.**

This document defines the Dealix proposal template — structure, pricing
guardrails, approval markers, and the rules that keep every proposal
defensible. Proposals are the most legally sensitive document we send.
They must read like a senior operator wrote them and a senior lawyer
reviewed them.

---

## 1. When to send a proposal

A proposal is sent after:

- A discovery call has happened (logged in the deal record).
- The buyer has named the outcome they want.
- The pricing band has been signaled and not rejected.
- A founder or senior owner has approved sending the proposal.

A proposal is **not** sent as a cold first touch. It is **not** sent
without the buyer's name, the buyer's company, and a specific scope.

---

## 2. Document structure

The canonical structure is nine sections. Each section is required unless
explicitly marked optional.

1. Cover
2. Context — what we heard
3. Outcome — what we will deliver
4. Scope — what is in, what is out
5. Approach — how we will do the work
6. Timeline — phase plan
7. Pricing — the band and terms
8. Trust — approval markers, data handling, references
9. Acceptance — signature lines

Optional sections may be added (case studies, sector data) only with
brand director sign-off and with proof-ledger references for any
referenced customer.

---

## 3. Section 1 — Cover

- Deep Navy background.
- Wordmark + tagline.
- Proposal title: "Proposal for [buyer company]".
- Proposal date.
- Prepared by: founder or owner name + email.
- Version: `v1`, `v2`, etc.
- One-line summary at the bottom of the cover: a sober sentence in
  Inter Medium, e.g. "14-day Pilot Sprint to ship the [outcome] for
  [buyer]."

The cover does not include marketing taglines, customer logos, or
"trusted by" graphics.

---

## 4. Section 2 — Context

A short prose section, 4–8 sentences, that:

- Names what the buyer told us.
- Names the constraint they are operating under.
- Names the desired outcome in the buyer's own words.

This section earns the buyer's attention by showing we listened. It is
**not** the place to make claims about Dealix.

Example opening: "On [date], [stakeholder] described a recurring pattern
where [outcome] stalled because [constraint]. The Pilot Sprint below
addresses that pattern directly."

---

## 5. Section 3 — Outcome

A bulleted list of 3–5 outcomes. Each outcome:

- Names a concrete artifact or signal.
- Includes a verification method (how we both know it shipped).
- Is bounded by the sprint window.

Example outcomes:
- "A deal map of [count] live [sector] accounts, signed by [buyer]."
- "An approval-queued outbound run with [count] drafts, with
  [buyer]'s manual approvals recorded."
- "A weekly evidence ledger entry, jointly signed at the close of each
  week."

The outcomes are what we will deliver. They are **not** revenue
guarantees. We do not write "30% more sales" here.

---

## 6. Section 4 — Scope

Two columns: **In scope** and **Out of scope**.

| In scope | Out of scope |
| --- | --- |
| Deal map for [sector] | CRM migration |
| Drafted outbound (with approval) | External sending without approval |
| Evidence ledger | Public case study (unless separately approved) |
| Weekly review | 24/7 support |

The "Out of scope" column protects the engagement. The buyer signs the
proposal knowing what they are not buying.

---

## 7. Section 5 — Approach

The approach section describes **how** the work happens, in 4–6 short
paragraphs:

- Discovery (what data we collect, with what consent).
- Drafting (what AI drafts, what humans approve).
- Approval queue (the trust gate).
- Execution (how external sends are recorded).
- Review (the weekly evidence ledger).

This is the section that names the trust gate explicitly. It is the
section that separates Dealix from scraping tools and generic agencies.

---

## 8. Section 6 — Timeline

A simple phase plan with dates. We use a small table.

| Phase | Days | Activity | Output |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 0 — Kickoff | Day 0–1 | Sign DPA, set up data | Signed DPA, data access |
| 1 — Map | Day 2–4 | Build deal map | Deal map artifact |
| 2 — Draft | Day 5–7 | Draft outbound | Drafted messages |
| 3 — Approve & Run | Day 8–11 | Buyer approval + execution | Approval ledger |
| 4 — Review | Day 12–14 | Review evidence | Signed ledger |

Timeline can be customized per buyer but always names the phases and
ends with a review.

---

## 9. Section 7 — Pricing

Pricing is governed by **strict guardrails**. Every proposal pulls from
the canonical offer ladder; bespoke pricing requires founder approval.

### 9.1 Sanctioned offers

| Offer | Scope | Band (SAR) | Term |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Pilot Sprint | 14 days, single use case | per offer ladder | Fixed |
| Pilot Extension | +30 days, second use case | per offer ladder | Fixed |
| Quarterly engagement | 90 days, multi use case | per offer ladder | Subscription |
| Annual engagement | 12 months, embedded | per offer ladder | Subscription |

(See `OFFER_LADDER.md` and `OFFER_LADDER_AND_PRICING.md` for the live
bands. This document does not duplicate those numbers; it references
them so the source of truth stays single.)

### 9.2 Pricing guardrails

- **No discounting below the floor** without founder approval logged
  in the proof ledger.
- **No outcome-based pricing** that ties our fee to a revenue/sales
  number. We do not promise outcomes; we do not bet our fee on them.
- **No "free pilot"** without an explicit, time-bounded grant signed by
  founder. A free pilot still requires a signed pilot agreement.
- **No multi-year commitments at single-year prices** without
  founder approval.
- **Payment terms** default to net-30 or up-front per the offer ladder.
  Custom terms require finance + founder approval.
- **VAT** is shown separately. ZATCA-compliant invoicing applies.

### 9.3 What pricing shows
- The offer name (matches the offer ladder).
- The included scope (matches Section 4).
- The price in SAR, with VAT shown separately.
- The payment terms.
- The validity window of this proposal (default: 14 days).

### 9.4 What pricing does not show
- Outcome guarantees ("30% lift").
- Conditional pricing tied to revenue or sales numbers.
- Hidden line items revealed later.

## 10. Section 8 — Trust

The trust section is where Dealix earns the contract. It contains:

- **Approval markers.** "Every external send passes through a human
  approval recorded in the approval ledger."
- **Data handling.** "We sign a DPA before any production data is
  shared. Data residency, retention, and access controls per the DPA."
- **Proof publication.** "We do not publish your name, logo, or
  results without your written approval."
- **References.** Listed only if the references have given written
  consent. Otherwise omitted.
- **Compliance.** PDPL alignment, ZATCA invoicing, any sector-specific
  notes.

This section is one of the most-read sections of the proposal. Keep it
specific and short.

---

## 11. Section 9 — Acceptance

The acceptance section has signature lines for both sides.

| For Dealix | For [Buyer] |
| --- | --- |
| Name: | Name: |
| Role: | Role: |
| Date: | Date: |
| Signature: | Signature: |

The proposal is countersigned via DocuSign / similar (or wet ink where
required). The signed PDF is filed in the proof ledger.

---

## 12. Approval markers — visible elements

To make the trust posture obvious to the buyer at a glance, the proposal
template includes small visible markers:

- **"Trust-gated execution" chip** in the header of Section 5.
- **"PDPL-aligned"** in the footer of Section 8.
- **"VAT shown separately"** in the footer of Section 7.
- **Version label** in the footer of every page.

These markers are not decoration. They are the buyer's at-a-glance
signal that this proposal will not surprise them later.

---

## 13. Bilingual proposals

For Arabic-speaking buyers, the proposal is delivered in Arabic with an
English appendix on request. The Arabic version:

- Is original copy, not literal translation.
- Uses IBM Plex Sans Arabic.
- Is RTL by default.
- Carries the same trust markers and pricing guardrails.
- Is reviewed by an Arabic-native reviewer before sending.

For mixed-language buying committees, we may deliver a bilingual proposal
(Arabic primary, English secondary) — labeled `_bil` in the filename.

---

## 14. Versioning

- `v1`: first delivered proposal.
- `v2`: any change after delivery, including scope adjustments, price
  changes, timeline adjustments.
- The version number sits in the cover and the page footer.
- Old versions are retained in the proof ledger; they are not deleted.

A buyer signing `v2` accepts `v2`. A buyer signing `v1` after `v2` was
issued is a process error — the owner should redraft `v3` for clarity.

---

## 15. Proposal review checklist

Before a proposal is sent, the owner runs this checklist:

- [ ] Buyer name, company, and contact correct.
- [ ] Context section reflects the actual discovery notes.
- [ ] Outcomes are artifacts or signals — not revenue guarantees.
- [ ] Scope and Out-of-scope columns are filled.
- [ ] Timeline is realistic; founder agrees we can deliver.
- [ ] Pricing matches the offer ladder.
- [ ] Trust section is complete.
- [ ] Acceptance lines present.
- [ ] No banned vocabulary (guaranteed revenue/sales/meetings).
- [ ] No unapproved customer references.
- [ ] Bilingual version prepared if buyer is Arabic-first.
- [ ] Approved by founder if pricing deviates from the ladder.

If any item fails, the proposal does not leave.
