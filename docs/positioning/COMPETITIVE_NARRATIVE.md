# Competitive Narrative

## 1. The three competitor archetypes

| # | Archetype | Example shape | Dealix counter |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Global CRM | Storage + reporting. | "CRM is the warehouse; Dealix is the factory." |
| 2 | Global outbound automation | High-volume sends, no trust gate. | "Trust gate or bust — every draft is approved." |
| 3 | Local agency | Manual, no operating system left behind. | "An agency is a project. Dealix is the system." |

## 2. The narrative we don't fight

- We don't fight CRMs on storage. We integrate.
- We don't fight outbound tools on send volume. We refuse the framing.
- We don't fight agencies on price. We sell the residual asset.

## 3. The narrative we win

- **Trust by construction** — every action is approval-gated and audited.
- **Saudi-native** — PDPL / ZATCA / sector rules baked in.
- **Founder console** — one operator surface, not five SaaS dashboards.
- **Compounding proof** — each closed deal becomes a sector report,
  case study, and outbound pattern.

## 4. The "why now" wedge

- Saudi B2B buyers are tightening governance, not loosening.
- LLM risks (prompt injection, excessive agency) are real and visible.
- Founders are tired of paying for tools that turn into liabilities.
- Compliance-aware, founder-controlled execution is the only stack
  that survives both the upside (AI leverage) and the downside (audit).

## 5. Battle-card line items

For every named competitor, the battle card states:

1. Their core promise (in their words).
2. The actual failure mode (in operator words).
3. One Dealix sentence that closes the gap.
4. A concrete proof artifact we can show.

The battle card never insults the competitor — that's a brand
violation. It states the operator-level truth and shows the proof.
