# Messaging Hierarchy

> Wordmark: **DEALIX**
> Tagline: **INTELLIGENT DEALS. REAL GROWTH.**
> Positioning: **Saudi B2B Revenue Operating System**

This document is the Dealix message house. It defines the top-line
message, the supporting pillars, the proof points that back each
pillar, and the CTAs that close each conversation. Every customer-
facing surface — site, deck, proposal, post, email — pulls from this
hierarchy. Surfaces do not invent new messages; they choose which
parts of this hierarchy to surface.

---

The top line is the single sentence we lead with. The pillars are the
five brand pillars plus three execution-specific extensions. Each
pillar carries 2–4 proof points. Every conversation ends with a
named CTA.

## 3. Pillars

The five brand pillars plus three execution extensions. Each pillar is
a stable unit; surfaces choose which to emphasize.

### Pillar 1 — Built on Trust
**The promise:** Every external action passes through a human approval
and is recorded in the evidence ledger.

**Proof points:**
- Trust gate visible in product UI.
- Approval ledger exportable.
- DPA signed before production data sharing.
- PDPL-aligned data handling.

### Pillar 2 — Driven by Growth
**The promise:** The product is built to move pipeline, not just track
it.

**Proof points:**
- Deal map artifact.
- Drafted outbound with approval.
- Sector-templated workflows.
- Weekly evidence review.

### Pillar 3 — Closing Deals
**The promise:** The verb that defines the company. Every artifact
serves the close.

**Proof points:**
- Pilot Sprint produces signed evidence ledger in 14 days.
- Approval queue prioritizes deals approaching close.
- Sector signal templates surface buying triggers.

### Pillar 4 — Focused on Results
**The promise:** Outcomes shown with denominators, not as hype.

**Proof points:**
- Metrics in customer-facing surfaces carry sample sizes.
- Sector reports disclose methodology.
- Customer proof published only with written approval.

### Pillar 5 — Global Mindset, Local Impact
**The promise:** World-class craft, Saudi-first execution.

**Proof points:**
- Saudi-first product architecture (Arabic-native, in-kingdom data).
- Bilingual surfaces with original Arabic copy.
- Founder operating in Saudi market.
- Sector reports specific to Saudi sub-sectors.

### Pillar 6 (execution) — Trust-Gated Execution
**The promise:** AI drafts, humans approve, the system records.

**Proof points:**
- Approval queue in product.
- Per-action approval ledger.
- No external send automation.
- "Draft Only" and "Approval Required" status chips.

### Pillar 7 (execution) — Defensible Claims
**The promise:** Nothing we publish — about ourselves or our customers —
cannot be defended.

**Proof points:**
- Customer references with logged written approval.
- Sector data with denominators and time windows.
- Methodology section in every report.
- "What we do not promise" slide in every deck.

### Pillar 8 (execution) — Operator-Built
**The promise:** Built by an operator for operators, not by a marketing
department.

**Proof points:**
- Founder operates in the Saudi B2B market.
- Product roadmap shaped by customer evidence.
- Sales narrative reflects real conversations, not generic playbooks.

## 5. Proof point patterns

Proof points follow a consistent shape so they can be slotted into any
surface.

**Pattern A — Product proof:** "The [feature] is visible in the
product. See screenshot or live demo."

**Pattern B — Evidence proof:** "Across [n] [unit] in [time window],
[observation]. Methodology: [reference]."

**Pattern C — Customer proof:** "[Customer], [role], [company]:
'[quote]'. (Published with written approval, [date].)"

**Pattern D — Compliance proof:** "[Compliance posture] under
[regulation], verified [date]."

Every proof point on every Dealix surface should fit one of these
four patterns. Anything that does not is either rhetoric or a claim
we cannot defend.

## 6. CTAs

Each surface ends with one named CTA. We do not stack CTAs.

| Surface | Primary CTA | Secondary CTA (optional) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marketing site hero | "Book a 30-minute discovery" | "Read the latest sector brief" |
| Sales deck (close) | "Sign the Pilot Sprint" | "Schedule a follow-up" |
| Proposal | "Countersign by [date]" | — |
| Sector report | "Talk to the analyst" | "Read more in [follow-up]" |
| LinkedIn essay | "DM me to discuss" | "Read the full brief" |
| Email outbound | "Reply with two times that work" | — |
| Email signature | (book a call link, sales variant only) | — |
| OG card | (no CTA; the click is the CTA) | — |

CTAs use plain verbs: "Book", "Sign", "Read", "Reply". They do not
use marketing verbs: "Unlock", "Discover", "Transform", "Get
started for free".

## 7. Top-line variants by audience

The top line stays the same; the surrounding emphasis shifts.

### 7.1 For a founder-led Saudi B2B (10–50 staff)
Lead with: "Dealix is the Saudi B2B Revenue OS. We give your team
the layer that holds your next ten conversations — drafts ready, your
approval gate, every action recorded."

### 7.2 For a mid-market revenue VP (50–200 staff)
Lead with: "Dealix is the Saudi B2B Revenue OS. We sit above your
CRM and turn it from a tracker into a mover — drafting, approving,
recording every external action."

### 7.3 For an enterprise division (200+ staff)
Lead with: "Dealix is the Saudi B2B Revenue OS. We give your
revenue function a trust-gated execution layer with an audit-ready
ledger — built for PDPL, ZATCA, and your internal audit
requirements."

### 7.4 For a partner / agency
Lead with: "Dealix is the Saudi B2B Revenue OS. We are the layer
that lets your client own the execution capability after your
engagement ends."

Each variant uses the same top line; the emphasis shifts to the
audience's primary frame.

## 8. Forbidden patterns

Off-limits in the message house, regardless of pillar:

- "Guaranteed revenue / sales / meetings" — any phrasing.
- "Promise" + revenue/sales/outcome.
- "10x growth" / "100x pipeline".
- "AI does it for you" / "hands-off outbound".
- "Trusted by the leading [...]" without named, approved customers.
- "Revolutionary" / "groundbreaking" / "best-in-class" applied to
  ourselves.

These patterns collapse the trust posture that the entire hierarchy
is built to protect.

---

## 9. The message house as a check

Before any sentence ships, ask:

1. Does it tie back to the top line?
2. Does it belong to one of the eight pillars?
3. Does it carry at least one proof point?
4. Does it use a banned pattern? If yes, revise.
5. Does the surface end with a single named CTA?

If a sentence fails (4), it is off-brand. If it fails (1–3), it may
be on-brand but it is not load-bearing — consider trimming.

---

## 10. Cadence of the top line

The top line is delivered once per surface, in full, near the top. It
is not delivered in every paragraph. Repetition without spacing reads
as marketing chant.

A surface delivers the top line and then earns it through the
pillars and proof points. The CTA closes the surface.

---

## 11. Approvals and stewardship

Changes to the top line require founder + brand director sign-off.

Changes to a pillar's promise require brand director sign-off and one
example proof point.

Changes to a proof point require the underlying evidence (with
denominator and source) before the proof point is published.

Changes are versioned by editing this document. Surfaces are updated
within one week of a hierarchy change.
