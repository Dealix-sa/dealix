# Saudi B2B Narrative

**Owner:** Founder
**Source of truth:** `docs/02_saudi_positioning/` + `docs/positioning/WHY_DEALIX_NOW.md`

## Frame

Saudi B2B is not a smaller version of US B2B. It is a different operating environment with different buyers, different data norms, different language requirements, and different velocity. A Revenue OS built for Saudi B2B must respect all four. A regional tool that adds Saudi as a region setting does not.

## The Saudi B2B buyer

The buyer Dealix is built for has these traits:

- **Founder or founder-equivalent.** Closes the last deal personally. Reads every outbound message before it ships.
- **Operator, not strategist.** Buys for cash impact within 60-120 days. Will not fund a 12-month "transformation."
- **Audit-conscious.** Has been burned by a black-box agency or a noisy SaaS contract. Will not delegate revenue without an audit trail.
- **Bilingual.** Communicates with customers in Arabic and English depending on segment. Expects vendors to do the same.
- **Locally referenced.** Buys from people who can show evidence of Saudi B2B work. National-level references matter more than global brand names.

## What this buyer rejects

- "AI-powered" generic pitches.
- Long sales cycles with multi-touch automation.
- Vendors who treat Arabic as an afterthought translation.
- Pricing that hides per-seat or per-meeting upcharges.
- Promises of "X leads per month."

## What this buyer values

- One named operator who owns the engagement.
- Sprint-shaped commitments with named outputs and dated proof.
- Bilingual artifacts where the Arabic is parallel, not condensed.
- Explicit exclusions ("we don't do X").
- A roadmap from Diagnostic to Sprint to Retainer that the founder can sign without lawyers.

## Saudi context dimensions

### Regulatory

- **PDPL (Personal Data Protection Law).** Dealix treats PDPL as a first-class governance layer. Data import, retention, redaction, and cross-border handling follow PDPL norms.
- **CITC (Communications, Space and Technology Commission).** Communication frameworks are honored — no bulk SMS, no unauthorized WhatsApp automation.
- **Sector regulators (SAMA, SDAIA, NCA, etc.).** Sector-specific data and AI governance is acknowledged in sector reports.

See `docs/02_saudi_positioning/PDPL_AWARE_LANGUAGE.md` and `docs/05_governance_os/`.

### Linguistic

- Arabic and English are both first-class. Not "English first with Arabic translation."
- Arabic body uses an Arabic-coverage font stack (see `DEALIX_TYPOGRAPHY.md`).
- Bilingual artifacts are parallel: same structure, same length (within 15 percent), same disclosures.

### Cultural

- Founder-to-founder relationships drive Saudi B2B introductions. Cold outreach without warm context is less effective than in other markets.
- Partner referrals through Saudi B2B integrators, consultancies, and event hosts carry disproportionate weight.
- Trust is built on observed delivery and dated proof, not on logo walls.

### Economic

- Vision 2030 reshapes enterprise spend distribution. Sectors like cybersecurity, ERP/CRM, logistics, B2B SaaS, and enterprise services see structural growth.
- Mid-market enterprises increasingly procure from Saudi-based vendors.
- Founder-led B2B service companies form at higher rates than in adjacent regional markets.

See `docs/intelligence/SAUDI_B2B_MARKET_MAP.md` for the sector taxonomy.

## What "Global Mindset, Local Impact" means

Global mindset:

- Use global craft standards for design, governance, and writing.
- Benchmark against Salesloft, HubSpot, Gong on category architecture.
- Recruit operators who would meet the bar at any global Revenue OS.

Local impact:

- Default to Saudi sectors, Saudi sample sizes, Saudi case studies.
- Build trust through Saudi partner relationships before regional or global expansion.
- Honor PDPL, CITC, and sector regulators as architecture, not afterthought.

The two work together. A global mindset without local impact is a tool no Saudi founder will buy. Local impact without global mindset is an agency that cannot scale.

## What Dealix specifically does for Saudi context

1. Sprint outputs include bilingual artifacts when the customer's audience is bilingual.
2. Account maps respect PDPL data provenance norms.
3. Outbound drafts respect CITC communication frameworks (no WhatsApp automation).
4. Proof Packs are anonymized by default; named cases require written customer authorization.
5. Sector reports are aggregated and methodology-led; never customer-identifying.
6. Founder communication defaults to the channel the buyer uses (often direct, often Arabic-led for first contact).

## Failure mode

- Dealix surfaces drift to English-first with Arabic as afterthought.
- A customer surface implies PDPL is a checkbox rather than architecture.
- A sector report cites an unnamed-but-identifiable customer.

## Recovery path

1. Re-publish surfaces with parallel Arabic.
2. Re-anchor PDPL and CITC language in customer artifacts.
3. Re-anonymize sector reports.
