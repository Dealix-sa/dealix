# Dealix Positioning Canvas

> Wordmark: **DEALIX**
> Tagline: **INTELLIGENT DEALS. REAL GROWTH.**
> Positioning: **Saudi B2B Revenue Operating System**

This document is the single positioning canvas for Dealix. It captures
the problem we name, the frame we use, the alternative we displace, the
promise we make, and the proof we attach. Every other positioning
document in this folder derives from this canvas.

A positioning canvas is not a slogan. It is the structured argument that
the rest of the company executes against.

## 2. Problem — what is broken

Saudi B2B teams in 2026 are operating in a market where:

- WhatsApp is the primary buyer channel.
- Saudi enterprise procurement demands evidence and audit.
- Vision 2030 sub-sectors are shifting buying patterns faster than
  global CRMs can track.
- The legacy stack (generic CRM + email + spreadsheets) was designed
  for a different market structure.

The result is a daily operational pattern we see across discoveries:

1. A salesperson has 15 active conversations across 4 channels.
2. The CRM record is days out of date.
3. The deal lives in WhatsApp threads that nobody else can see.
4. The board asks for evidence; the team produces a slide that
   nobody believes.
5. The next quarter starts with the same gap.

**The problem we name** is not "you need a better CRM". It is "you do
not have an operating layer that holds signals, approvals, and
evidence in one place — and your existing tools were not built to be
that layer."

---

## 3. Frame — how we see the problem

The frame is the angle from which we look at the problem. Frames win
sales conversations because they reshape what the buyer thinks the
problem **is**.

Three frames we choose:

### 3.1 Execution > Tracking
Most CRMs frame revenue as a **tracking** problem. We frame it as an
**execution** problem. Tracking is what you do after the work; we
focus on the work itself — drafting, approving, sending, recording.

### 3.2 Trust gate > Automation
Most martech frames the trust gate as friction to be removed. We
frame the trust gate as the feature that protects the brand, the
relationship, and the regulator. Removing the trust gate is what
made the previous generation of tools untrustworthy.

### 3.3 Saudi-first > Localized
Most tools are built globally and localized for Saudi. We are built
**Saudi-first**: Arabic-native UI, Riyadh data residency, sector
templates that reflect Saudi buying behavior. Localization is a
patch; Saudi-first is a foundation.

---

## 4. Alternative — what they currently use

Buyers compare us, by default, to four alternatives. Naming them
honestly is part of the positioning.

| Alternative | What it does well | Why it breaks for Saudi B2B |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Scraping tools | Find contacts fast | Cold data, no approval, brand risk |
| Spam / blast tools | Reach volume | Regulator risk, brand damage |
| Generic CRM (HubSpot, Salesforce, Zoho) | Track pipeline | Tracker, not mover; English-first |
| Generic agency | Outsource execution | Opaque, no audit trail, no system |

We are not "better than" any of these alone. We are a different
**layer**: the layer above the tools, where intelligence, approval,
and evidence live.

This framing matters because it removes the buyer's apples-to-apples
comparison. We do not compete with their CRM; we sit on top of it.

## 5. Promise — what we commit to

The promise is what we are willing to put our name to. It is bounded,
specific, and defensible.

**The Dealix promise:**

> We will give your Saudi B2B revenue team a trust-gated operating
> layer that drafts the work, routes it through your approvals,
> records every approved action, and surfaces evidence for your board
> — without ever sending an external message you have not approved.

What the promise does **not** include — and we say so explicitly:

- We do not promise a specific revenue, sales, or meeting count.
- We do not promise a specific time to close.
- We do not promise outcomes that depend on the buyer's actions
  outside the system.

This non-guarantee is part of the positioning. It is the most
counter-intuitive and most defensible line we say.

---

## 6. Proof — what we show

Positioning without proof is rhetoric. Our proof stack:

### 6.1 Product proof
- The approval queue is visible inside the product.
- The evidence ledger is exportable.
- The trust gate cannot be bypassed by a user role.

### 6.2 Pilot proof
- The 14-day Pilot Sprint produces a deal map, drafted outbound
  with approvals, and a signed evidence ledger.
- Each pilot output is a concrete artifact, not a slide deck.

### 6.3 Customer proof
- Cited customers carry written approval logged in the proof ledger.
- Uncited customers remain anonymous.
- Metrics carry denominators ("n=X, Q-Y").

### 6.4 Methodology proof
- Sector reports disclose sample, window, exclusions.
- Forward-looking statements use cautious language.

### 6.5 Compliance proof
- PDPL-aligned data handling.
- ZATCA-compliant invoicing.
- DPA signed before production data is shared.

## 9. The buyer the positioning is built for

The positioning addresses the buyer in the Saudi B2B founder / GM / VP
Revenue chair. Sub-profiles:

- **Founder-led B2B (10–50 staff).** Closing deals personally;
  wants the layer that holds the next ten conversations.
- **Mid-market B2B (50–200 staff).** Has a CRM, has lost faith in
  it; wants the layer that turns CRM into a movement system.
- **Enterprise B2B division (200+).** Has a CRM and a tooling
  budget; wants the trust-gated execution layer that satisfies
  internal audit.

These three buyers respond to the same positioning canvas with
different emphasis. The trust gate matters most for enterprise; the
operating layer matters most for mid-market; the deal map matters most
for founder-led.

## 10. Buyer they are not

We do **not** position for:

- B2C / consumer revenue teams.
- Pure marketing teams without revenue accountability.
- Companies who want a hands-off auto-outbound tool.
- Companies who refuse to sign a DPA.

A buyer who does not fit is told so. A bad-fit pilot costs us more than
a no.

---

## 11. Positioning anti-patterns

Avoid:

- "Dealix is like HubSpot but for Saudi." — collapses the category.
- "Dealix is AI-powered." — true and meaningless; everything is.
- "Dealix transforms your revenue." — banned vocabulary.
- "Dealix is the future of B2B." — banned vocabulary.

Use instead:

- "Dealix is the Saudi B2B Revenue OS."
- "Trust-gated execution."
- "We draft. You approve. We record."

## 12. Positioning validation

We validate the positioning quarterly by:

- Asking 10 Saudi B2B operators to describe Dealix back to us in
  one sentence. The closer their sentence to our canonical
  positioning, the better the system is working.
- Reviewing every customer-facing surface for alignment.
- Reviewing competitor positioning shifts.
- Reviewing recorded sales calls for which phrases land and which
  do not.

Where the positioning is being misread, we adjust the surfaces, not
the canvas. The canvas changes only with founder + brand director
sign-off.

---

## 13. What changes the canvas

The canvas changes when one of these is true:

- The buyer's primary pain shifts (e.g. compliance becomes more
  acute than execution).
- The competitive landscape shifts (e.g. a Saudi-built CRM emerges).
- The product surface expands beyond the current scope.
- The category vocabulary loses defensibility because competitors
  adopt it.

When the canvas changes, every dependent doc (`COMPETITIVE_NARRATIVE.md`,
`MESSAGING_HIERARCHY.md`, etc.) is updated in the same week.
