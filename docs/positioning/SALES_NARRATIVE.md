# Sales Narrative

> Wordmark: **DEALIX**
> Tagline: **INTELLIGENT DEALS. REAL GROWTH.**
> Positioning: **Saudi B2B Revenue Operating System**

This document describes the Dealix sales narrative arc — the way we
conduct a sales process from the first touch to the signed engagement
and the follow-up. It is the operator-level playbook that complements
the messaging hierarchy (`MESSAGING_HIERARCHY.md`), the objection
handling (`OBJECTION_HANDLING.md`), and the positioning canvas
(`DEALIX_POSITIONING.md`).

The narrative is broken into six phases, each with its own opening
move, mid-conversation pattern, and close. It is bilingual where the
buyer is Arabic-first.

---

## 1. The six phases

1. **Opening** — the first touch.
2. **Discovery** — the qualification conversation.
3. **Demo** — the working session that shows the layer.
4. **Proposal** — the document that closes the conversation.
5. **Close** — the countersignature and kickoff.
6. **Follow-up** — what happens after Day 14.

Each phase has a goal, a structure, and a list of forbidden moves.
The narrative is not a script; it is a discipline.

---

## 2. Phase 1 — Opening

### 2.1 Goal
Earn a 30-minute discovery call. Not a sale, not a demo, not a
proposal. Just the discovery call.

### 2.2 Channel
Warm intro is the strong default. Cold opening is allowed only when
the cold message can name a specific, defensible reason for contact
(a sector observation, a recent buyer signal, an existing
relationship in the buyer's network).

### 2.3 Opening message structure (warm)
- One sentence on why we are reaching out (the warm intro, the
  observation).
- One sentence on what Dealix is in operator terms.
- One sentence on what the next step would be.

Example, English:

> [Name], [intro] suggested we connect. We are building Dealix —
> the Saudi B2B Revenue OS — and your work in [sector] has been
> exactly the kind of context we want to learn from. Open to a
> 30-minute call next week? Tue 10:00 or Wed 14:00 Riyadh both
> work for me.

Example, Arabic:

> الأخ [الاسم]، [الاسم المشترك] اقترح علينا التواصل. نَبني
> دِيليكس — نظام تشغيل الإيرادات للأعمال السعودية B2B — وعملك
> في قطاع [القطاع] هو السياق الذي نَتعلّم منه. هل ٣٠ دقيقة
> الأسبوع القادم تَناسبك؟ عندي وقتان: الثلاثاء ١٠:٠٠ أو
> الأربعاء ١٤:٠٠ بتوقيت الرياض.

### 2.4 Opening message structure (cold)
- One sentence naming the specific signal that prompted the
  message (a sector report, a hiring move, a published interview).
- One sentence on what Dealix is.
- One sentence on the next step.

Cold without a named, specific signal is forbidden.

### 2.5 Forbidden opening moves
- Mass outbound to a scraped list.
- "We can help you 10x revenue."
- "Guaranteed meetings booked."
- "Quick question..." (the buyer has heard this 10,000 times).
- Sending an attachment in the opening message.

### 2.6 Opening close
The opening close is two named times. Not "let me know when works".
Two specific times in the next 5–10 business days.

## 3. Phase 2 — Discovery

### 3.1 Goal
Understand the buyer's actual revenue situation, qualify the fit,
and decide together whether a demo or a proposal is the right next
step.

### 3.2 Duration
30 minutes is the default. We hold it to 30 minutes unless the
buyer extends it. Long discovery calls are a buyer-fit signal, not
a sales-skill signal.

### 3.3 Structure

| Minutes | Activity |
| --- | --- |
| 0–3 | Introductions, frame the call |
| 3–18 | Buyer-side: what they are seeing, what they have tried |
| 18–25 | Dealix-side: what we are, what we are not |
| 25–30 | Mutual next step + scheduling |

### 3.4 Opening frame (script-equivalent)

> Thanks for the 30 minutes. The goal is to understand your situation
> and decide together whether what we are building fits. I will spend
> most of the time listening and 5–7 minutes describing Dealix. If by
> the end the fit is not obvious, that is fine — we will both have
> learned something.

This opening reduces pressure and signals operator-to-operator
respect.

### 3.5 Discovery questions (in priority order)

1. "What does your revenue function look like today — team size,
   tools, who closes?"
2. "Where does the deal stall most often?"
3. "Where does evidence live when the board asks?"
4. "What does your current AI / outbound exposure look like?"
5. "If we ran a 14-day Pilot Sprint, what would the most useful
   artifact be?"
6. "What would make this a clear no for you?"

The last question is the most important. It surfaces objections
early.

### 3.6 What we say about Dealix (5–7 minutes)
- The category in one sentence: "Saudi B2B Revenue OS".
- The three nouns: drafts, approves, records.
- The trust gate: no external send without approval.
- The Pilot Sprint: 14 days, three artifacts, signed ledger.
- The non-guarantees: no revenue/sales/meeting promises.

Use the same vocabulary every time so the buyer hears the category
repeated.

### 3.7 Forbidden discovery moves
- Talking more than the buyer.
- Demoing the product without being asked.
- Skipping the "clear no" question.
- Promising revenue numbers.
- Mocking the buyer's existing CRM, agency, or tooling.

### 3.8 Discovery close
At minute 28, three options:

1. "Based on what you said, we are a fit. I will send a proposal by
   [date]. Want to schedule a 20-minute proposal walk-through?"
2. "Based on what you said, a working session would be helpful next
   — 60 minutes, with me and 1–2 of your team, looking at your
   actual pipeline. Want to schedule that?"
3. "Based on what you said, the fit is not obvious to me. Let me
   send a short note tomorrow with what I would recommend instead."

Option 3 is real. We do not propose to bad-fit buyers.

## 4. Phase 3 — Demo (working session)

### 4.1 Goal
Show the layer working with the buyer's actual situation — not a
canned demo. Convert the conversation from "interesting" to
"specific".

### 4.2 Duration
60 minutes. Two attendees from the buyer side ideally.

### 4.3 Structure

| Minutes | Activity |
| --- | --- |
| 0–5 | Welcome, set the working frame |
| 5–10 | Review one of the buyer's live accounts together |
| 10–30 | Walk through the deal map, drafted outbound, approval queue |
| 30–45 | Walk through the evidence ledger |
| 45–55 | Buyer-driven exploration |
| 55–60 | Next step |

### 4.4 Critical move
We do not run a feature tour. We run a working session anchored on
the buyer's situation. The product is the substrate; the buyer's
context is the focus.

### 4.5 What we never demo
- A guaranteed-revenue dashboard. We do not have one.
- An "AI sends emails automatically" feature. We do not have one.
- A "trusted by" customer logo wall.
- A fake customer's real data.

### 4.6 Demo close
At minute 58, ask: "Based on what we walked through, is the Pilot
Sprint the next step?" The answer is yes, no, or "let me think".
All three are acceptable. We do not pressure.

---

## 5. Phase 4 — Proposal

### 5.1 Goal
Send the document that, when signed, starts the engagement. The
proposal does not negotiate; it codifies.

### 5.2 Structure
Per `DEALIX_PROPOSAL_TEMPLATE_GUIDE.md`. Cover, context, outcome,
scope, approach, timeline, pricing, trust, acceptance.

### 5.3 Delivery
The proposal is sent within 48 hours of the demo, in PDF, with a
short email cover. The email cover:

> Per our session, the Pilot Sprint proposal is attached.
> Highlights:
>
> - 14 days, [start date] to [end date].
> - Three artifacts: deal map, drafted outbound, signed evidence
>   ledger.
> - [SAR band], net-30, VAT separate.
>
> The proposal is valid for 14 days from today. Happy to walk
> through it on a 20-minute call if useful.

### 5.4 Forbidden proposal moves
- Discounting without founder approval.
- Changing the offer ladder for one buyer.
- Adding "guaranteed outcomes" to please a stakeholder.
- Removing the "what we do not promise" section.

### 5.5 Proposal close
After 5 business days without countersignature, send one polite
nudge. After 10 business days, send a close-out: "If now is not
the right time, I will close this and check in next quarter."

We do not chase forever.

---

## 6. Phase 5 — Close

### 6.1 Goal
Get the countersignature, sign the DPA, and start Day 0 of the
Pilot Sprint.

### 6.2 Sequence
1. Buyer countersigns the proposal.
2. DPA is signed in parallel (often same day).
3. Internal kickoff is scheduled for within 5 business days.
4. Day 0 of the Pilot Sprint begins.

### 6.3 Internal communication
The deal record is updated. The proof ledger is opened. The
delivery team is briefed. The founder approves the kickoff.

### 6.4 Forbidden close moves
- Skipping the DPA to start faster.
- Promising scope additions to clinch the close.
- Starting Day 0 before the proposal is countersigned.

### 6.5 Close ritual
A short message from the founder to the buyer:

> Thank you. We start [Day 0 date]. The kickoff call is on [date].
> The first artifact lands on [date]. The signed ledger lands on
> [Day 14 date]. Talk soon.

Calm, specific, no hype.

---

## 7. Phase 6 — Follow-up

### 7.1 Goal (engaged buyer)
Renew or expand the engagement on the strength of the signed
evidence ledger, not on hype.

### 7.2 Timing
The follow-up conversation happens within 5 business days of the
end of the Pilot Sprint, ideally with the signed ledger in hand.

### 7.3 Structure

| Minutes | Activity |
| --- | --- |
| 0–5 | Re-cap the ledger together |
| 5–20 | What worked, what did not |
| 20–35 | What the buyer wants next |
| 35–45 | Renewal or expansion options |
| 45–50 | Next step |

### 7.4 Renewal options
- Continue Pilot Sprint (second sector / use case).
- Move to quarterly engagement.
- Move to annual engagement.
- Pause and revisit next quarter.

Pausing is a legitimate option. We do not pressure to extend.

### 7.5 Goal (lost buyer)
Maintain the relationship. The buyer who said no this quarter may
say yes next year. Add them to a low-volume quarterly check-in.

### 7.6 Goal (ghosting buyer)
Send one close-out note ("I am closing this out — happy to revisit
next quarter") and stop. Chasing damages the brand.

## 8. The narrative voice across phases

The voice stays consistent across phases:

- **Calm.** No urgency, no pressure.
- **Specific.** Named times, named artifacts, named dates.
- **Operator-to-operator.** No "as a sales professional" framing.
- **Bilingual where it matters.** Switch language when the buyer
  switches.
- **Trust-gated.** Mention the approval queue, the evidence
  ledger, and the non-guarantee posture often.

A consistent voice across six phases compounds into trust.

## 9. Cadence and SLAs

| Action | SLA |
| --- | --- |
| Reply to inbound | 1 business day |
| Send proposal after demo | 2 business days |
| Send DPA on request | 1 business day |
| Nudge after proposal silence | 5 business days |
| Close-out after proposal silence | 10 business days |
| First Pilot Sprint artifact | Day 4 |
| Signed evidence ledger | Day 14 |
| Follow-up after Day 14 | 5 business days |

Missing an SLA is a process bug. Log it; fix it.

---

## 10. What we do not do in sales

- Pursue a bad-fit buyer to make a quarter number.
- Discount below the floor to clinch a close.
- Add scope that we cannot deliver in 14 days.
- Promise outcomes we cannot defend.
- Publish a customer name in a follow-up without approval.
- Use a tracking pixel in plain reply emails.
- Send 5 nudges over 30 days.

## 12. Closing reminder

The sales narrative is not a script. It is a discipline. The
disciplined operator wins more Saudi B2B deals over time than the
scripted closer wins in any single quarter.

Calm, specific, trust-gated, bilingual, sober. That is the Dealix
sales voice across all six phases.
